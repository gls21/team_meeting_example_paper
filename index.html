False Light: Legal Mobilization Among Moths Against Deceptive Lure-Based Technologies

Grace Skinner*, Abra Ash*,
UKCEH
*Indicates Equal Contribution
Paper Code Supplementary

Abstract

In recent decades, artificial light traps have become a widespread method for studying or eliminating moth populations. However, emerging evidence suggests that moths are not passive victims of this technology. This paper presents a satirical yet data-driven analysis of an unprecedented behavioral shift: collective legal awareness among moth populations. Leveraging ethological observations, semiotic analysis, and fictional insect jurisprudence, we document how repeated exposure to UV light traps—often falsely signaling "moonlight" or "nectar nearby"—has led to coordinated efforts by moths to organize class-action lawsuits on the grounds of false advertising. Preliminary motions filed by Actias luna and Autographa gamma include charges of psychological distress, wing singeing, and existential betrayal. We conclude that, while unenforceable in human courts, the moths’ legal resistance illuminates broader questions of multispecies consent, ethical illumination design, and justice in the age of ecological manipulation.

Keywords: phototactic injustice, lepidopteran legalism, deceptive luminance, collective insect litigation, light trap ethics, nocturnal class action
